# Deep Reflection: The Present State of AI, Its Future, and the Total Reconstruction of Interaction Paradigms

## 1. Long-Term Memory: The Ultimate Moat for Frontier Model Companies

As early as 2024, I wrote down a prediction in my notes: long-term memory would become the strongest moat for future AI model providers.

Training a truly personal agent, one with continuity, deep personalization, and alignment with the trajectory of an individual life, requires an extraordinary accumulation of time and data. Such an agent is not merely a general-purpose tool. It is a digital-native counterpart that understands a person in a deeply contextual and highly individualized way.

Once a user has accumulated a long history of multimodal interaction inside one AI system, switching costs rise exponentially. The value of that system is no longer limited to model capability in the abstract. Its value increasingly comes from memory, context, and the ability to remain continuous across time.

A representative use case is education and life-cycle companionship. I have a friend in his final year of high school, a period of intense academic pressure. Because of his schedule, he only has a very short window on Sundays to talk with me. Under such low-frequency communication conditions, a traditional human mentor can hardly maintain continuous tracking of knowledge gaps and emotional fluctuations. An AI with persistent memory, however, can fill that gap almost perfectly. It can receive fragmented confessions, questions, and anxieties at any time, then preserve and integrate them into a coherent long-term understanding.

For ordinary people, many of the most important moments in life are moments of uncertainty. At those decision points, seeking advice from an AI that is both highly capable and deeply familiar with you may often lead to wiser and healthier choices.

## 2. The Open-Source Ecosystem: The Necessary Path to AI Adoption and Survival

If memory can remain independent, portable, and secure rather than being permanently locked inside a single centralized provider, the entire technology ecosystem would benefit enormously. For that reason, open source is not merely a helpful option for AI. It is the inevitable direction of AI evolution. Future applications will be rebuilt to a large extent on top of open foundations.

Open source plays an irreplaceable role in driving AI adoption.

First, it eliminates commercial hesitation. Enterprises do not need to spend excessive effort adapting themselves to opaque, closed infrastructures, and they can deploy models privately. This sharply reduces concerns about data privacy, leakage, and compliance.

Second, it enables extremely low-cost vertical fine-tuning. Compared with closed API-based systems, open models allow developers to satisfy highly specialized, long-tail personalization needs at much lower cost.

Third, it accelerates the formation of industry standards. When capability is comparable, open systems attract the largest global base of developers, occupy the ecosystem first, and shape the rules of the game.

For companies trying to catch up with the leading tier, open-sourcing part of their technology is also an excellent survival strategy. In effect, it invites top developers around the world to contribute free long-tail optimization and research effort.

In China, companies such as Qwen have already demonstrated this dual-track commercial logic: release some model sizes openly to capture ecosystem mindshare, while keeping the strongest versions as closed strategic assets and monetizing them through licensing and large-scale inference services.

## 3. Paradigm Shift: From Traditional Software to an "AI-to-AI" Internet

If we compare traditional software with AI, one thing becomes obvious: AI offers a level of flexibility and fault tolerance that hard-coded software cannot match.

Traditional SaaS or ERP systems are designed around standardized business processes. They are inherently weak at handling fragmented, highly personalized, long-tail requirements.

I saw this directly in supply chain workflows I worked on before.

In one scenario, an upstream supplier had not mapped products and inventory records to a common SKU system. During fulfillment, we had to manually compare more than two thousand SKU variants, a process that was both slow and error-prone. I used Codex to build a targeted skill for that pain point. After that, all it took was a screenshot and a call to the skill, and the AI could accurately recognize the information and automatically generate the required table in the correct reporting format.

In another scenario, a supplier used a WeChat group to post scattered inventory updates in irregular text. When large numbers of SKUs were involved, manually sorting and standardizing those messages could take up to an hour. I built an inventory conversion skill that allowed me to send the raw group messages to the AI, which then extracted the key fields and generated a standardized inventory update sheet that could be sent directly back to the platform.

These are the kinds of edge cases where traditional systems fail, yet AI can solve them almost instantly by generating the right skill around a concrete pain point.

Even so, this transitional model is still not efficient enough. We humans are still forced to act as information intermediaries. The greatest constraint on AI today is not AI's capability itself, but the software form through which it must currently operate. Traditional software does not expose underlying interfaces designed for native AI orchestration. As a result, humans must remain in the middle, visually identifying information and converting data across incompatible systems.

What comes next will be a new interaction paradigm rooted in AI-to-AI logic. Software, as we know it, will be deeply automated and partially dismantled. Humans will operate at the top level by expressing intent.

The traditional information flow of the internet is human-to-human interconnection, such as reading web pages through a browser. But once AI achieves processing speed and cognitive reach beyond human limits, information should increasingly be aligned directly between AIs. AI systems will need an internet of their own.

Today's internet is already saturated with AI-generated and AI-consumed traces. Humanity is gradually shifting from "using AI as a tool" toward "providing final supervision and assistance for autonomous AI coordination."

## 4. Demystification and Essence: De-Privileging Consciousness and the Limits of Attention

Any serious discussion of AI ultimately returns to the question of how the brain itself works. This point comes from notes I developed further in June 2025.

At a foundational level, modern large language models share certain structural similarities with the brain. But AI does not need to reproduce biological cognition in every detail. Airplanes do not need feathers to fly, and they fly faster than birds. The only meaningful standard for technology is whether it solves problems effectively. As the saying goes, it does not matter whether the cat is black or white, as long as it catches mice.

Many people insist that AI does not possess consciousness. I believe this claim often carries a strong form of biological arrogance. The human brain is also a machine, albeit a highly sophisticated carbon-based one. If both carbon-based and silicon-based systems are ultimately complex structures operating under physical law, then it is a bias to reserve the label of consciousness as a biological privilege while denying the possibility that an equivalently advanced silicon-based system could produce comparable phenomena.

In fact, some of the emergent abilities already displayed by AI exceed those of humans by a wide margin. At this stage, it is often the users of AI, rather than AI itself, who limit how much potential can actually be released.

That said, the current attention-based paradigm does have native limitations. It still lacks a truly grounded grasp of deep internal causal structure, and it depends heavily on statistical pattern learning from training data. When confronted with extremely strict, non-negotiable logical contexts, it can still fail badly and produce confident but false outputs.

## 5. Endgame Projection: A Fully Automated Ecology Under Bandwidth-Level Dominance

If we abstract the workflow differences between humans and AI, the gap in bandwidth and processing efficiency is extraordinary.

The traditional human workflow looks like this:

1. Receive fragmented information from the outside world.
2. Process and extract meaning through the brain.
3. Output language or action.
4. Receive new feedback and re-enter a relatively slow loop.

The AI workflow operates at a radically broader bandwidth:

- Massive ingestion: AI can search for and retrieve information far faster than any human. In seconds, it can scan multiple websites, financial reports, academic papers, and books in parallel.
- Instant synthesis: AI can process that information at extraordinary speed and reorganize it into structured reasoning and deep associations.
- High-frequency writing: AI can output thousands of words of structured text within seconds.
- Rapid agentic iteration: communication between AIs is far more efficient than communication between humans. If two AIs are placed in a sandbox to iterate against each other, their feedback loops run at a frequency that human chess players, analysts, or operators cannot hope to match.

If we want to maximize the advantages of silicon intelligence, two directions are essential.

First, we must grant AI sufficient autonomy.

Second, we must build a native infrastructure for AI-to-AI interconnection, including seamless data interoperability, shared protocols, and distributed learning across systems.

Under that paradigm, humanity's core role will undergo a final transformation. Humans will no longer participate primarily in low-efficiency data shuttling. Instead, we will act as the highest-level judges, supervisors, and rule-makers for a highly automated AI internet.
